Graph Basics for Ramsey Theory

Definitions first; then interact with each object directly.

1. Paths \(P_n\)

Definition. The path \(P_n\) has vertices \(v_1,\dots,v_n\) and edges \(v_iv_{i+1}\) for \(i=1,\dots,n-1\). (So \(P_3\) is a 3-vertex chain of length 2.)
Notation tip: we often call \(n\) the order (number of vertices). Paths have two degree-1 endpoints and all other degrees 2.

2. Cycles \(C_n\)

Definition. The cycle \(C_n\) (\(n\ge 3\)) is \(P_n\) plus the closing edge \(v_nv_1\).
Every vertex has degree 2. The length is \(n\).

3. Complete graphs \(K_n\)

Definition. The complete graph \(K_n\) has all \(\binom{n}{2}\) possible edges among \(n\) vertices.
Cliques are the “densest” simple graphs; \(K_3\) is a triangle.

4. Complete bipartite graphs \(K_{m,n}\)

Definition. Partition the vertices into two parts of sizes \(m,n\). Connect every vertex of one part to every vertex of the other; no edges inside a part. The result is \(K_{m,n}\).
Special case: the star \(K_{1,3}\) has one center joined to three leaves.

5. Disjoint unions: \(2K_3\) and \(mK_n\)

Definition. \(2K_3\) is the disjoint union of two triangles; \(mK_n\) is the disjoint union of \(m\) copies of \(K_n\).
We use these often in Ramsey statements like \(R(2K_3,K_3)=8\).
